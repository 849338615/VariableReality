Four Displays.
Four Different Realities.

Frame Rate
How many still images flash per second to create the illusion of motion. Lower rates feel choppy; higher rates feel hyper-smooth.
White Point Shift
A warm white point tints everything amber. A cool white point adds blue. Same pixels, but your brain adapts to each as if it were neutral.
Saturation / Vivid Mode
Boosted saturation makes colors more intense than in the original. Many displays ship with "vivid" modes on by default to look impressive in stores.
Contrast & Black Level
A high-contrast display crushes dark shadow detail. A lifted black level makes blacks look gray. Both change what you can and cannot see.

Frame rate (measured in fps) determines how frequently a display refreshes the image. Film uses 24 fps for a cinematic look. Most phones refresh at 60 or 120 fps, which is why scrolling feels smoother on newer devices.

Higher frame rates don't change what's shown, just how often. But that difference affects everything from gaming responsiveness to how natural a video call feels.

24 fps · Cinema60 fps · Standard144 fps · Gaming

White point is measured in Kelvin (K). A candle is about 1,900K. Daylight is roughly 6,500K. Many office monitors default to 9,300K, which adds a blue tint that most people never notice.

Your brain performs "chromatic adaptation," adjusting its perception of white within minutes. That's why night mode (3,000K) looks orange for 30 seconds, then feels normal.

4,000K · Warm6,500K · Neutral9,300K · Cool

Display saturation is tied to color gamut coverage. An sRGB monitor at 100% coverage reproduces web-standard colors accurately. Many consumer panels cover 110-130% sRGB, which makes colors appear more vivid than intended.

Samsung's "Vivid" mode can push effective saturation to 130%+ sRGB. Professional displays like the EIZO ColorEdge or BenQ SW series let you clamp to exactly 100% sRGB for accurate proofing.

72% sRGB · Budget TN99% sRGB · Calibrated125%+ sRGB · Vivid mode

Contrast ratio is the difference between the darkest black and brightest white a display can produce. A typical IPS laptop panel achieves about 1,000:1. OLED screens can exceed 100,000:1.

Low contrast means dark scenes look washed out. Very high contrast can crush subtle shadow gradients into pure black. Both extremes hide information.
